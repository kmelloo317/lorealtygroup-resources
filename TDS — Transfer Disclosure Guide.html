Understanding the Transfer Disclosure Statement

A simple guide to help you understand what you're signing and what it means for you.

What is the TDS?

The Real Estate Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS) is one of the most important documents in a California home sale. Required by law for most residential transactions, it's the seller's written disclosure of everything they know about the condition of the property — defects, improvements, features, and more. As a buyer, reading this carefully can save you from major surprises after you move in.

California Civil Code requires sellers of residential property (1-4 units) to provide a TDS to buyers before the sale closes. This is not optional — it's the law.

The purpose of the TDS California follows a "buyer beware" principle, but with an important exception — sellers must proactively disclose what they know. The TDS is the mechanism for doing that. It protects buyers from hidden surprises and protects sellers from claims of fraud or concealment after the sale.

There are limited exemptions — such as REO (bank-owned) properties, court-ordered sales, and certain probate transactions. In those cases, an Exempt Seller Disclosure (ESD) is used instead.

The TDS asks sellers to disclose all known material facts about the property — anything that could affect the value or desirability of the home in the eyes of a reasonable buyer.

  • Known defects in the structure, roof, foundation, plumbing, electrical, or HVAC systems
  • Any unpermitted additions, conversions, or improvements
  • Presence of water damage, mold, or pest infestations
  • History of insurance claims or significant repairs
  • Neighborhood nuisances such as noise, odors, or encroachments
  • HOA membership, fees, and any pending special assessments
  • Any legal disputes, liens, or easements affecting the property
  • Deaths on the property within the past three years (with limited exceptions)
Sellers must disclose what they know Sellers are not required to investigate issues they're unaware of — but they cannot intentionally hide known problems. If a seller discovers an issue and fails to disclose it, they can be held legally liable even years after the sale.

The TDS includes a section completed by the listing agent based on their own visual inspection of the property. Both the listing agent and the buyer's agent are required under California Civil Code to conduct and document a visual inspection of accessible areas.

Agent Visual Inspection Disclosure (AVID) The AVID is a separate form where agents document what they observed during their walkthrough. This is in addition to the seller's TDS and provides an independent layer of disclosure from the agent's perspective.

Agents are not home inspectors and are not expected to identify issues that require specialized knowledge. Their job is to note what's visible and reasonably apparent during a normal walkthrough.

When you receive the TDS, read every section carefully — don't just skim it. Pay particular attention to any "Yes" answers or written explanations, as these flag areas that warrant follow-up.

  • Look for any items checked "Yes" in the defects section and read the explanation
  • Note any systems or features the seller marks as unknown — this may mean they haven't maintained them
  • Cross-reference the TDS with the Seller Property Questionnaire (SPQ) for a more complete picture
  • Take note of anything the seller writes in the additional comments section
  • Compare what the seller discloses with what you observed during your showing
Ask questions before removing contingencies If anything in the TDS is unclear or concerning, ask your agent to get clarification from the seller before you remove your inspection contingency. Once contingencies are removed, your options become more limited.

Some disclosures are more serious than others. These are the ones that typically warrant additional investigation or expert review.

  • Foundation issues: Cracks, settling, or movement can be expensive to repair and affect the structural integrity of the home
  • Water intrusion or mold: Past or ongoing moisture problems can signal deeper issues and potential health concerns
  • Unpermitted work: Additions or conversions done without permits may need to be permitted retroactively — or removed
  • Roof age or damage: A roof near end of life can mean significant costs shortly after purchase
  • Neighborhood disputes or encroachments: Ongoing disputes with neighbors can become your problem after you close
  • HOA issues: Pending special assessments, litigation, or financial problems with the HOA can affect your costs and the property's value

None of these are necessarily deal-breakers — but each one deserves a closer look, often from a licensed specialist.

Once you receive the TDS, you have the right to investigate the disclosed items further before committing to the purchase.

3-day right to cancel In California, buyers have 3 days after receiving the TDS (or 5 days if delivered by mail) to cancel the contract for any reason related to the disclosures — even if you've already signed the purchase agreement. This right exists regardless of your other contingency periods.

You can also use the TDS disclosures as the basis for a Request for Repair (RR) — asking the seller to fix issues, provide credits, or reduce the price in response to what was disclosed.

If you have concerns about specific systems or areas of the home, this is also the time to schedule specialist inspections — roof, foundation, sewer, chimney, and so on.

If you discover a material defect after closing that was not disclosed and that the seller knew about, you may have legal recourse.

Document everything If you discover an undisclosed defect, document it thoroughly with photos, contractor estimates, and any evidence suggesting the seller was aware of it. This documentation will be essential if you pursue a legal claim.

California law allows buyers to seek damages from sellers who knowingly failed to disclose material defects. The statute of limitations varies, so if you believe you have a claim, consult a real estate attorney promptly.

Keep in mind that not every post-closing discovery is grounds for a claim. If an issue was visible during inspections or could have been discovered through reasonable due diligence, it may be harder to hold the seller responsible.
