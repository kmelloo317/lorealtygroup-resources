Understanding the Contingency Removal

A guide to one of the most important decisions in your home purchase

✅

What is the CR?

The Contingency Removal (CR) is the form a buyer signs to formally release one or more contingencies in the purchase agreement. Once you remove a contingency, you're committing to move forward with the purchase under those terms. It's one of the most consequential decisions in the home buying process — and understanding exactly what you're giving up before you sign is essential.

1
What a Contingency Removal Means
Paragraph 14 — What you're committing to when you sign this form
Buyer Important

When you sign a Contingency Removal, you are telling the seller — in writing — that you are satisfied with that aspect of the transaction and are prepared to move forward. It's not a casual form. It carries real financial consequences if you later try to back out.

Once removed, your deposit is at risk

After you remove contingencies, canceling the contract without a valid reason means the seller may be entitled to keep your deposit as liquidated damages — typically up to 3% of the purchase price. Don't remove contingencies until you are genuinely ready to commit.

2
The Three Main Contingencies
Paragraphs 3L, 3E, 3B — Inspection, loan, and appraisal
Buyer Important

The standard California purchase agreement includes three primary contingencies, each of which can be removed separately or together.

  • Inspection Contingency — Gives you the right to investigate the property and cancel if you find unsatisfactory conditions. Removing this means you accept the property in its current inspected condition.
  • Loan Contingency — Protects you if your lender denies your loan. Removing this means you're committing to close even if financing falls through — a significant risk if your loan isn't fully approved.
  • Appraisal Contingency — Protects you if the home appraises below the purchase price. Removing this means you'll cover any gap between the appraised value and your purchase price out of pocket.
3
Active vs. Passive Contingency Removal
Paragraph 14C — The two ways a contingency can be removed
Buyer

In California, contingencies can be removed in two ways — actively by the buyer, or passively through a seller-initiated process.

Active removal

You proactively sign and deliver the Contingency Removal form, releasing one or more contingencies on your own timeline. This is the most straightforward approach.

Passive removal via Notice to Perform

If you miss a contingency deadline, the seller can issue a Notice to Buyer to Perform (NBP), giving you a short window (typically 2 days) to either remove the contingency or cancel. If you don't respond, the seller may have the right to cancel the contract.

4
Removing the Inspection Contingency
Paragraph 14 — What to confirm before signing off
Buyer

Before removing your inspection contingency, make sure you have completed all investigations you intend to do — and that you're comfortable with what you found.

  • General home inspection completed and report reviewed
  • Specialist inspections completed (roof, foundation, sewer, chimney, pool, etc.)
  • Repair requests submitted and seller's response received and accepted
  • Permit history reviewed with local building department
  • HOA documents reviewed if applicable
  • Homeowner's insurance confirmed as available and affordable

Don't remove early to be competitive

In competitive markets, some buyers feel pressure to remove contingencies quickly to make their offer more attractive. Understand the risks before agreeing to shortened timelines — cutting corners on inspections can lead to costly surprises after closing.

5
Removing the Loan Contingency
Paragraph 3E — What your lender needs to have confirmed
Buyer Important

The loan contingency is your safety net if your financing falls through. Before removing it, you should have strong confidence that your loan will close.

  • Full loan approval (not just pre-approval) received from your lender
  • Appraisal has been ordered and completed if required by your lender
  • No major changes to your financial situation (new debts, job changes, large purchases)
  • Closing timeline confirmed with your lender

Pre-approval is not the same as loan approval

Pre-approval means a lender has reviewed your basic financials and believes you qualify. Full loan approval means the loan has been underwritten and conditionally approved for the specific property. Don't remove your loan contingency based on pre-approval alone.

6
Removing the Appraisal Contingency
Paragraph 3B — What it means if the home appraised low
Buyer Important

If you remove the appraisal contingency and the home appraises below your purchase price, you are committed to making up the difference in cash.

For example, if you're purchasing a home for $900,000 and it appraises at $860,000, you would need to bring an additional $40,000 to closing beyond your planned down payment. Make sure you have the financial capacity to absorb this before removing this contingency.

When removing makes sense

In competitive markets, buyers sometimes offer to waive or remove the appraisal contingency to make their offer stronger. This strategy makes the most sense when you have significant cash reserves and are willing to pay above appraised value for the right home.

7
Partial vs. Full Contingency Removal
Paragraph 14 — Removing one contingency vs. removing all of them
Buyer

You don't have to remove all contingencies at once. The CR form allows you to remove specific contingencies while keeping others in place.

For example, you might remove your inspection contingency after completing your due diligence while keeping your loan contingency in place until your lender gives full approval. This is a common and sensible approach that protects you while demonstrating good faith to the seller.

Communicate with your agent

Before signing any Contingency Removal, have a conversation with your agent about what you're giving up and what you're keeping. This is one of the most important decisions in the transaction — take the time to understand it fully.
