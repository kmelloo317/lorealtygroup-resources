Understanding the Seller Property Questionnaire

A guide to one of California's most important disclosure documents

📝

What is the SPQ?

The Seller Property Questionnaire (SPQ) is a detailed companion to the Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS). While the TDS covers general property condition, the SPQ goes deeper — asking the seller specific questions about the property's history, systems, permits, insurance claims, and any known issues. Together, the TDS and SPQ form the core of the seller's disclosure package in California.

1
Why the SPQ Exists
California Civil Code 1102 — The disclosure obligation behind this form
Both

The TDS gives buyers a broad overview of property condition. The SPQ fills in the details — asking targeted questions that the TDS doesn't cover. Together they give buyers the most complete picture possible of what they're purchasing.

Required in most transactions

Most California purchase agreements require the seller to complete both the TDS and the SPQ. Buyers should read both documents together for a full picture of the property.

2
What the SPQ Covers
Sections A–F — The six categories of questions you'll answer
Seller Important

The SPQ is organized into categories that cover the property's history in detail.

  • Structural systems — foundation, roof, walls, windows, and drainage
  • Mechanical systems — plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and appliances
  • Water and moisture — any history of leaks, flooding, or water damage
  • Permits and improvements — whether additions or renovations were permitted
  • Insurance claims — any claims filed during the seller's ownership
  • Legal and financial — liens, easements, HOA disputes, or pending assessments
  • Environmental — presence of asbestos, lead paint, mold, or underground storage tanks
  • Neighborhood — noise, odors, traffic, or other conditions affecting the property
3
Permit History and Unpermitted Work
Section C — What to disclose about additions and improvements
Both Important

The SPQ asks sellers to disclose whether any improvements, additions, or conversions were made without permits. This is one of the most consequential sections for buyers.

Unpermitted work is your responsibility after closing

If you purchase a home with unpermitted work, you inherit the liability. The city may require you to retroactively permit the work, bring it up to current code, or in some cases remove it entirely. Always verify permit status with the local building department during your inspection period.

Common unpermitted items

Garage conversions, room additions, patio covers, pool equipment, electrical panel upgrades, and HVAC replacements are among the most frequently unpermitted improvements in residential properties.

4
Insurance Claims History
Section D — Prior claims, repairs, and known damage
Buyer

The SPQ asks sellers to disclose any insurance claims made during their ownership. This can reveal patterns of damage that may not be visible today.

A history of roof claims may indicate recurring leaks. Water damage claims could point to ongoing moisture issues. Fire or smoke damage claims may have structural implications even if repairs were made. This information can help you decide which specialist inspections to prioritize.

Request the CLUE report

A Comprehensive Loss Underwriting Exchange (CLUE) report shows the insurance claim history for a property going back 7 years. You can request this from the seller or their insurance company as part of your due diligence.

5
HOA and Legal Disclosures
Section E — Association rules, fees, and pending disputes
Buyer

If the property is in a homeowners association, the SPQ asks about dues, pending special assessments, and any active disputes or litigation involving the HOA.

Special assessments can be significant

A pending special assessment — for example, to repair a shared roof or repave a parking lot — can add thousands of dollars to your costs shortly after closing. Always ask for current HOA financials and meeting minutes during your inspection period.

The SPQ also asks about any legal disputes, boundary issues, or easements affecting the property. These can have lasting effects on your use and enjoyment of the home and should be reviewed carefully.

6
Environmental Disclosures
Section F — Mold, lead, asbestos, and other hazards
Both

The SPQ includes questions about known environmental hazards on or near the property.

  • Asbestos — common in homes built before 1980, often found in insulation, flooring, and popcorn ceilings
  • Lead-based paint — required disclosure for homes built before 1978
  • Mold — any known presence or past remediation
  • Underground storage tanks — old fuel tanks from prior uses of the property
  • Soil contamination — especially relevant for properties near former industrial sites

Older homes require extra attention

Homes built before 1980 are more likely to contain asbestos and lead paint. Both are manageable if undisturbed, but renovation or removal can be costly. Factor this into your due diligence and budget planning.

7
Reading the SPQ as a Buyer
Sections A–F — What to look for and what follow-up questions to ask
Buyer

When you receive the SPQ, read it alongside the TDS. Look for consistency between the two documents — any discrepancies may indicate incomplete disclosure.

  • Pay close attention to any "Yes" answers and the explanations provided
  • Note any questions marked "Unknown" — this may indicate deferred maintenance or lack of oversight
  • Use the SPQ to prioritize which inspections and specialists to hire
  • Ask your agent for clarification on anything that concerns you before removing contingencies

The SPQ is a disclosure document, not a warranty. The seller is telling you what they know — your job as a buyer is to investigate further and verify what matters most to you.
