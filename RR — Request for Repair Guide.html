Understanding the Request for Repair

A guide to negotiating repairs after your inspection

🔧

What is the RR?

The Request for Repair (RR) is the form a buyer uses to formally ask the seller to address issues discovered during the inspection period. It's not an ultimatum — it's the start of a negotiation. Understanding how to use this form strategically, and what to expect in return, can make a significant difference in the outcome of your transaction.

1
When to Submit a Request for Repair
Timing within the inspection contingency period
Buyer

The Request for Repair must be submitted while your inspection contingency is still active — typically within the first 17 days of the contract. Submitting it early gives both parties time to negotiate without the pressure of an expiring deadline.

Don't wait until the last minute

Submitting a repair request one or two days before your contingency expires leaves very little time for the seller to respond and for you to consider your options. Plan to submit your request as soon as you've completed your inspections and reviewed the results.

2
What to Include in a Repair Request
How to frame your requests effectively
Buyer

The most effective repair requests are clear, specific, and focused on legitimate concerns rather than wish lists.

  • Reference the specific inspection report and item number where possible
  • Describe the issue clearly — what it is, where it is, and why it matters
  • Specify what you're asking for — a repair, a credit, or a price reduction
  • Attach relevant inspection report pages to support your request
  • Focus on health, safety, and major systems rather than cosmetic items

Avoid overreaching

Requesting too many small or cosmetic repairs can frustrate the seller and put the deal at risk. Focus on the issues that genuinely matter — structural concerns, safety hazards, and major system failures.

3
What You Can Request
Repairs, credits, or price reductions
Buyer

Buyers have three basic options when submitting a repair request — and the right choice depends on the situation.

Option 1: Repairs

You ask the seller to fix specific items before closing. The seller hires contractors and completes the work. Best for issues where the fix is straightforward and you want it resolved before you move in.

Option 2: Credit

The seller gives you a credit at closing — either toward your closing costs or as a reduction in the purchase price — instead of making repairs. Best when you'd prefer to choose your own contractors or handle repairs on your own timeline after moving in.

Option 3: Price Reduction

You ask the seller to lower the purchase price to account for the cost of repairs. Similar to a credit but structured differently and requires a contract amendment.

4
The Seller's Response Options
What to expect after you submit
Both

Once you submit a Request for Repair, the seller has several ways to respond — and no legal obligation to agree to any of your requests.

  • Agree to all requests — the seller agrees to make all requested repairs or provide the requested credits
  • Agree to some requests — the seller accepts certain items and declines others
  • Counter with a lesser credit — the seller offers a smaller credit than requested
  • Decline entirely — the seller refuses to make any repairs or offer any credits

If the seller declines, you still have options. You can accept the property as-is, counter-negotiate, or cancel the contract and recover your deposit — as long as your inspection contingency is still active.

5
Negotiating After the Seller Responds
How to approach the back and forth
Both

A repair negotiation is exactly that — a negotiation. It's rare for the first request to be the final agreement. Approach it with flexibility and keep the big picture in mind.

Keep the deal in perspective

If you've found a home you love in a competitive market, consider what truly matters to you in the repair negotiation. Fighting over small items can damage goodwill and put the entire transaction at risk over things that cost very little to fix yourself after moving in.

Your agent will help you evaluate the seller's response and advise on a reasonable counter. The goal is to reach an agreement that both parties can accept — not to "win" the negotiation.

6
Documenting the Agreement
Getting it in writing
Both Important

Any repair agreement reached between buyer and seller must be documented in writing — either through the seller's formal response form or an addendum to the purchase agreement.

Verbal agreements don't count

If the seller verbally agrees to make repairs but you don't have it in writing before you remove your contingencies, you have no recourse if the work isn't done at closing. Always document repair agreements formally before signing the Contingency Removal.

Before closing, you have the right to do a final walkthrough to verify that agreed-upon repairs were completed. If they were not, you have the right to address this before releasing funds.

7
Final Walkthrough and Verification
Confirming repairs before you close
Buyer

Within 5 days of closing (or sooner if agreed), you have the right to do a final walkthrough of the property to confirm its condition and verify that any agreed-upon repairs were completed.

  • Walk through every room and inspect all systems that were repaired
  • Request receipts and permits for any work that was done
  • If repairs were not completed as agreed, notify your agent immediately — do not simply close and hope for the best
  • If issues are found, you may be able to negotiate a credit at closing, delay closing, or in some cases cancel the transaction

The walkthrough is not a second inspection

The final walkthrough is not an opportunity to identify new issues or renegotiate the deal. It's specifically to confirm that the property is in the agreed-upon condition and that repair commitments have been fulfilled.
