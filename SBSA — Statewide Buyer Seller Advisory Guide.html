Understanding the Statewide Buyer and Seller Advisory

A comprehensive guide to critical topics in California real estate

💡

What is the SBSA?

The Statewide Buyer and Seller Advisory (SBSA) is one of the longest documents in a California real estate transaction — and one of the most valuable. It's a comprehensive overview of topics that every buyer and seller should be aware of, from natural hazards and insurance to inspections and environmental issues. This guide breaks down the most important sections so you know what to pay attention to.

What the SBSA Is and Why It Exists
Required Advisory — Why this form is part of every transaction
Both

The SBSA is not a disclosure of specific property conditions — it's an educational document. It advises buyers and sellers about issues and factors that could affect the value, safety, or desirability of residential property in California. It's provided by your agent and should be read carefully before you make key decisions in your transaction.

Advisory, not disclosure

The SBSA advises you to investigate and consider various factors — it does not tell you whether those factors apply to your specific property. That investigation is your responsibility during the due diligence period.

2
Natural Hazards
Section I — Flood zones, fire zones, fault lines, and more
Both Important

California has a wide range of natural hazard zones that can affect property safety, insurance availability, and value. The SBSA advises buyers to research whether a property is located in any designated hazard zone.

  • Fire Hazard Severity Zones (FHSZ) — high-risk areas for wildfire, which affect insurance availability and cost
  • Flood zones — areas with elevated risk of flooding, which may require flood insurance
  • Earthquake fault zones — proximity to active faults, which affects seismic risk
  • Landslide and liquefaction zones — areas prone to ground movement during earthquakes
  • Tsunami inundation zones — coastal areas at risk from tsunamis

A Natural Hazard Disclosure (NHD) report — provided by a third-party company — will confirm which, if any, of these zones apply to the specific property.

3
Homeowner's Insurance
Section IV — Why coverage is getting harder to find in California
Buyer Important

The SBSA strongly advises buyers to investigate homeowner's insurance early in the process — before removing contingencies. Insurance availability and cost in California have changed dramatically in recent years.

Don't assume you can get coverage

Many major insurers have stopped writing new policies in California, particularly in wildfire-prone areas. Some properties are very difficult or extremely expensive to insure. Verifying that you can obtain adequate coverage at a reasonable cost is a critical part of your due diligence — not an afterthought.

  • Contact multiple insurers early in your inspection period
  • Ask specifically about wildfire, earthquake, and flood coverage
  • Review the cost of insurance in your monthly budget calculations
  • If coverage is unavailable through standard carriers, ask about the California FAIR Plan as a last resort
4
Environmental Hazards
Section III — Mold, lead, asbestos, and contamination
Both

The SBSA advises buyers about a range of environmental hazards that may be present in or near the property.

  • Lead-based paint — common in homes built before 1978; requires a separate federal disclosure
  • Asbestos — found in insulation, floor tiles, and other materials in older homes
  • Mold — can cause health issues and indicates moisture problems that need to be addressed
  • Formaldehyde — found in some building materials and can affect air quality
  • Radon — a naturally occurring radioactive gas that can accumulate in homes
  • Methamphetamine contamination — former drug manufacturing sites may require expensive remediation

Specialized inspections are available

Standard home inspections do not test for most environmental hazards. If you have concerns about any of these issues, request a specialized inspection from a qualified environmental professional during your contingency period.

5
Property and Neighborhood Conditions
Section II — Noise, odors, traffic, and local nuisances
Buyer

The SBSA advises buyers to investigate a wide range of neighborhood and property conditions that may not be visible during a showing.

  • Noise — airports, freeways, train tracks, schools, or commercial activity nearby
  • Odors — agricultural operations, wastewater treatment, or industrial facilities in the area
  • Traffic — volume, patterns, and planned road changes that could affect the property
  • Sex offender registry — publicly available information on registered offenders in the area
  • Zoning and land use — planned developments, commercial encroachment, or zoning changes that could affect the neighborhood
  • Schools — research district boundaries and school ratings independently

Many of these factors are subjective — what matters to one buyer may not matter to another. Use the SBSA as a checklist of things to investigate and decide based on your own priorities and lifestyle.

6
Legal and Title Issues
Section V — Easements, encroachments, and zoning
Both

The SBSA advises buyers about legal matters that can affect property ownership and use.

  • Easements — rights held by others to use a portion of the property (utility easements, access easements, etc.)
  • CC&Rs — covenants, conditions, and restrictions that limit how you can use the property, common in planned communities
  • Property line disputes — boundary disagreements with neighbors
  • Liens — financial claims against the property that must be resolved before or at closing
  • Title insurance — the importance of understanding what your policy covers and what it excludes

Review the preliminary title report

The preliminary title report (prelim) is provided early in the transaction and outlines the current state of title — including any existing liens, easements, or encumbrances. Review this document carefully with your agent and ask questions about anything you don't understand.

7
Inspections the SBSA Recommends
Section VI — What to inspect and why
Buyer

The SBSA strongly encourages buyers to conduct thorough investigations of the property beyond a standard general home inspection. It specifically recommends considering the following.

  • Roof inspection — age, condition, and remaining life of the roofing system
  • Sewer inspection — scope the lateral line to check for roots, breaks, or blockages
  • Foundation inspection — look for signs of settling, movement, or structural damage
  • Chimney inspection — safety and condition if the home has a fireplace
  • Pool and spa inspection — equipment, structure, and safety compliance
  • Pest inspection — termites, wood-destroying insects, and fungal damage
  • HVAC inspection — age, efficiency, and remaining life of heating and cooling systems

Each specialist inspection costs a few hundred dollars — a modest investment relative to the cost of discovering a major problem after closing.

8
Wire Fraud Warning
Section VII — How to protect yourself from escrow fraud
Both Important

The SBSA includes a critical warning about wire fraud — one of the most common and costly scams in real estate transactions.

Never wire money based on emailed instructions alone

Criminals intercept real estate email communications and send fraudulent wiring instructions that appear to come from your escrow company, agent, or lender. If you receive wiring instructions by email, always call a known, verified phone number to confirm before sending any funds. A mistake here can result in the permanent loss of your down payment or closing funds.

  • Always verify wiring instructions by phone — call a number you have independently verified, not one from the email
  • Be suspicious of any last-minute changes to wiring instructions
  • Your escrow company will never pressure you to wire funds immediately without verification
  • Report any suspected fraud immediately to your bank and to law enforcement
