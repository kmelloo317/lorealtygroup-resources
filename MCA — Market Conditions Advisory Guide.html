What is the MCA?

The Market Conditions Advisory (C.A.R. Form MCA, Revised 6/24) is a two-page disclosure that explains the risks of buying or selling real estate in a changing market. It covers how hot and cool markets work, the risks of waiving contingencies, and what it means to write offers on multiple properties. Both buyers and sellers sign it to confirm they received and understood this information before moving forward.

The MCA opens by explaining something most people know instinctively but don't always factor into their decisions: real estate markets are cyclical. They go up, they go down, and nobody can predict with accuracy where they'll be in six months.

Hot Market vs. Cool Market In a "hot" market there are more buyers than sellers. Competition drives prices up and buyers often offer over asking price or waive contingencies to win. In a "cool" market there are more sellers than buyers, prices can level off or drop, and buyers have more negotiating power.

The form also notes that foreclosures and short sales (called "distressed" properties) are factored into how brokers and appraisers value other homes in the area. If distressed sales are happening nearby, they can push comparable values down even for non-distressed homes.

Key Takeaway for Buyers The value of the home you purchase may not increase over time and could actually decrease. This is especially relevant if you're buying at or above asking price in a competitive market. Only you can decide if the price is right for your situation.

The form is direct on this point: as a buyer, you are responsible for the price you choose to offer. We can pull comparable sales data from the MLS to help you understand the market, but that data is often delayed. Prices can change faster than reported sales reflect, so comps from even a few weeks ago may not tell the full story.

  • If your offer is accepted, the property's value may not increase and could decrease
  • If your offer is accepted, you might later feel you paid too much (buyer's remorse)
  • If your offer is rejected, there's no guarantee you'll find a similar property at the same price
  • If your offer is rejected, you may look back and question whether the amount was right for you

Only you can decide whether your offer makes sense given the property, the market, and your personal circumstances. Our job is to give you the best information available so you can make that call with confidence.

Most purchase agreements include contingencies that give you a window to cancel the transaction without penalty if specific conditions aren't met. In competitive markets, buyers sometimes waive these protections to make their offer more appealing. Sellers may even require it. The MCA spells out exactly what you're giving up if you go that route.

The Four Main Contingencies Loan Contingency: Protects you if you can't secure financing. Appraisal Contingency: Protects you if the home appraises below the purchase price. Investigation Contingency: Protects you if you're dissatisfied with the property's condition after inspections. Insurance Contingency: Protects you if you can't obtain homeowners insurance at an acceptable cost.
If You Remove a Contingency and Then Can't Perform You may be in default under the contract. That means you could be required to pay damages to the seller or forfeit your deposit entirely. Removing contingencies carries real financial risk. The MCA states clearly that writing a non-contingent offer is acting against our advice as your broker.

One important note on the investigation contingency: even if you waive it, the seller may still be legally required to disclose material facts about the property. In some cases, California law gives you an independent right to cancel for a limited time after receiving those disclosures, even without a contingency in place.

We recommend against writing a non-contingent offer, even if you plan to pay all cash. If you decide to proceed with one anyway, the MCA outlines three things we strongly encourage you to do first:

  • Review all available seller reports, disclosures, and documents before submitting the offer
  • Have a qualified professional inspect the property, even if it's being sold "as-is"
  • Carefully assess your financial position and risk with your attorney, accountant, or financial advisor
The Bottom Line The decision to write a non-contingent offer is yours alone. We can advise you, walk you through the risks, and help you prepare, but only you can decide how much risk you're willing to accept. We are not able to make that decision for you.

In competitive markets or during short sale situations, some buyers submit offers on multiple properties even though they intend to buy only one. The MCA addresses this directly because it carries real legal risk that many buyers aren't aware of.

While making offers on multiple properties isn't illegal, you can find yourself legally bound to more than one seller if more than one accepts your offer. If you then cancel without using a valid contingency, a seller may claim you fraudulently induced them into the contract, even if you still have standard contingencies remaining.

The Risk of Not Disclosing If you are writing offers on multiple properties and have not disclosed this to each seller, and you later cancel without a contingency that specifically covers this situation, you could be exposed to a breach of contract claim. This applies even when you have other standard contingencies still in place. Talk to us before submitting multiple offers so we can help you navigate this properly.

The MCA's final section addresses sellers directly. As a seller, you are responsible for setting your asking price. We can provide comparable sales data from the MLS, but just as with buyers, that data is often delayed. Prices can move faster than reported sales reflect, in either direction.

There is no guarantee that your asking price, or the price you ultimately agree to accept, is the highest price the market would have paid for your property. Pricing is a decision only you can make, informed by the data and advice we provide but not determined by us.

What Your Signature Means By signing the MCA, both buyers and sellers confirm they have read, understood, and received a copy of this advisory. You are not agreeing to any particular price or outcome. You are acknowledging that we reviewed these market realities with you before your transaction moved forward.
