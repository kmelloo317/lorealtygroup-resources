Understanding the Square Footage and Lot Size Advisory

A simple guide to help you understand what you're signing and what it means for you.

📐

What is the SFLS?

The Square Footage and Lot Size Advisory and Disclosure (C.A.R. Form SFLS, Revised 12/24) is a one-page form that puts buyers and sellers on notice that square footage and lot size figures in real estate listings are not always accurate. There is no single official source and no standard method for measuring these numbers. The form discloses whatever figures are available and where they came from, and strongly advises buyers to verify independently rather than rely on the listing.

Important: This guide is for informational purposes only and is not legal advice. The SFLS is part of your legally binding transaction documents. Always read the full document and consult a qualified attorney if you have questions before signing.

The SFLS opens with a straightforward statement: measurements of structures vary from source to source, data is often contradictory, and there is no one official size or standard method for calculating square footage. This isn't a problem unique to any one property. It's a structural issue across the industry.

Common Sources and Their Limitations Public Record / County Assessor: May be based on older permit data or estimates that were never field-verified. Multiple Listing Service (MLS): Often copied from prior listings or county records without independent verification. Seller's Own Measurement: Based on whatever method the seller or their contractor used, which may not match appraisal standards. Appraisal: Typically the most reliable, as appraisers follow standard measurement guidelines and measure in person. Survey, Architectural Drawings, Condo Map: Highly accurate for what they measure, but may not reflect subsequent changes.

The form is direct about what this means for buyers: do not rely on any advertised or disclosed square footage figure. If square footage matters to your decision or your price-per-square-foot calculations, retain your own expert, such as a licensed appraiser, to verify it during your investigation period.

Price Per Square Foot The form specifically calls out that price-per-square-foot calculations are broad estimates that can vary significantly by location, property type, and amenities. If you are using this figure to evaluate price, have the square footage independently verified first. An error of even 100 to 150 square feet can meaningfully change your analysis.

Lot size carries the same reliability problem as home square footage. Fences, hedges, walls, and retaining walls are visual markers, but they often don't align with legally defined property boundaries. Existing structures or amenities may also fall outside the actual property lines or violate local setback requirements without either party knowing.

When Lot Size Really Matters If the size or configuration of the lot is important to your decision, or if you're planning to build an ADU, add a pool, expand the structure, or use specific areas of the property, the form advises you to hire a licensed surveyor. A licensed surveyor is the only professional who can legally and accurately determine lot dimensions, boundary locations, and acreage. Do not rely on listing data or county records alone for any plan that depends on a specific lot dimension.
  • Fences and walls may not follow legal property lines
  • Improvements like sheds, pools, or additions may not be within the actual property boundaries
  • Easements for utilities, drainage, or neighboring access can reduce your usable area without being visible on site
  • Only a licensed surveyor can provide a legally accurate determination of boundaries and acreage

The SFLS is clear on this point: brokers and agents do not have expertise in determining exact square footage and lot size. We have not and will not verify the accuracy of any numerical statements regarding square footage, room dimensions, lot size, or the location of property boundaries.

This isn't a limitation we've chosen — it's a professional boundary that exists because the accurate determination of these figures requires specialized expertise (licensed appraisers for square footage, licensed surveyors for lot boundaries) that is outside the scope of real estate practice.

What We Will Do We can help you understand what figures are available and where they come from. We can recommend qualified professionals to verify the numbers. We can also flag situations where a discrepancy between sources is significant enough that you should investigate before removing contingencies. What we cannot do is independently verify or guarantee any figure.

Paragraph 4 is where the form discloses the actual numbers. Any square footage or lot size figures are entered alongside the source they came from. The form lists up to ten possible sources, and any figures are noted as approximations only. Other figures from other sources may also exist.

What Buyer's Signature Confirms By signing, you acknowledge that you read and received the SFLS. The form includes a strong caution in capital letters: if any measurement is material to your decision and you do not independently verify it, you are acting against the advice of your brokers and agents. Do not sign and then assume the listed figures are accurate. Investigate during the contingency period, not after closing.

The seller's signature confirms they are not aware of any other measurements of the property beyond what is disclosed in the table, and that they have read and received their copy of the form.
